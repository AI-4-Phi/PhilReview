# Literature Review Plan: Social Experiments in the Agentic Economy

## Research Idea Summary

This project applies social experimentalism (Adams, Himmelreich) to agentic marketsâ€”markets where AI agents transact on behalf of humans. Using the Magentic Marketplace simulation platform, the research treats simulated agentic markets as social experiments for moral and institutional learning, investigating how procedural features (transparency, fairness constraints, adaptive regulation) affect legitimacy and fairness when decision-makers are artificial but represent human interests.

## Key Research Questions

1. Can procedural standards of fairness or accountability be realized and evaluated within agentic markets?
2. What counts as procedural justification when decision-makers are non-human but represent human interests?
3. Do agentic markets exhibit moral pathologies analogous to those in human institutions, such as bias, manipulation, and epistemic injustice, and can procedural reforms mitigate them?
4. Can social-experimental reasoning be extended to hybrid systems of human and AI agents, yielding new criteria for legitimate automation?

## Literature Review Domains

### Domain 1: Social Experimentalism and Procedural Justification in Political Philosophy

**Focus**: Theoretical foundations of social experiments as sources of normative justification, particularly the Adams-Himmelreich framework and related work on procedural experimentalism in political philosophy.

**Key Questions**:
- What is social experimentalism and how does it differ from traditional approaches to normative justification?
- How do procedural features of experiments ground moral learning and legitimate principles?
- What is the relationship between social experiments, democratic experimentalism (Dewey, Sabel), and evidence-based policy?
- What are the criteria for a valid social experiment in normative theory?
- How does experimentalism handle disagreement, uncertainty, and value pluralism?

**Search Strategy**:
- Primary sources: Recent work by Adams, Himmelreich on social experiments; SEP articles on "Dewey" (experimentalism), "Political Legitimacy"
- Key terms: ["social experiments", "procedural justification", "experimentalism", "democratic experimentalism", "normative learning", "moral learning", "experimental political philosophy"]
- Expected papers: 15-20 key papers
- Inclusion criteria: Papers explicitly theorizing experiments as sources of normative justification, not merely as empirical inputs to pre-established theory

**Relevance to Project**: This is the core theoretical framework. Understanding how experiments justify principles is essential for arguing that Magentic simulations can serve as legitimate sites of normative learning.

### Domain 2: Procedural Justice, Legitimacy, and Fair Markets

**Focus**: Theories of procedural justice, legitimacy, and fairness in markets and economic institutions, including mechanism design, market ethics, and theories of economic justice.

**Key Questions**:
- What makes a market procedure fair (not just outcomes)?
- How is legitimacy established in market institutions?
- What are the procedural requirements for fair exchange, negotiation, and allocation?
- How do transparency, accountability, and voice function as procedural values in economic contexts?
- What is the relationship between procedural and distributive justice in markets?

**Search Strategy**:
- Primary sources: SEP articles on "Procedural Justice", "Distributive Justice", "Justice and Bad Luck"; PhilPapers category "Philosophy of Economics"; recent work in market design ethics
- Key terms: ["procedural justice", "market legitimacy", "fair exchange", "mechanism design ethics", "economic proceduralism", "market design", "institutional fairness"]
- Expected papers: 12-18 key papers
- Inclusion criteria: Papers on procedural (not just outcome-based) standards for markets; ethical analyses of market mechanisms

**Relevance to Project**: Establishes normative criteria for evaluating agentic market procedures. What counts as a fair agentic negotiation depends on theories of procedural fairness.

**Focus**: Moral status, agency, and responsibility in AI systems; algorithmic fairness; ethics of autonomous agents acting on behalf of humans.

**Key Questions**:
- What moral considerations apply to AI agents acting as economic proxies?
- How should we understand agency, autonomy, and responsibility when agents are artificial?
- What is algorithmic fairness and how does it relate to traditional theories of justice?
- How do problems of bias, transparency, and accountability manifest in autonomous systems?
- What are the ethical requirements for delegation to AI agents?

**Search Strategy**:
- Primary sources: SEP articles on "Artificial Intelligence" (ethics section), "Robot Ethics"; PhilPapers category "Philosophy of Computing and Information"; leading AI ethics journals (Ethics and Information Technology, AI & Society)
- Key terms: ["AI ethics", "autonomous agents", "algorithmic fairness", "artificial moral agency", "AI proxy", "algorithmic accountability", "machine ethics", "AI delegation"]
- Expected papers: 15-20 key papers
- Inclusion criteria: Papers addressing normative questions about AI as decision-makers or representatives, not purely technical ML fairness papers

**Relevance to Project**: Core to RQ2 (procedural justification with non-human decision-makers) and RQ3 (moral pathologies in artificial systems). Connects general AI ethics to specific economic contexts.

- How do simulations relate to other empirical methods (experiments, surveys, case studies)?

**Search Strategy**:
- Primary sources: Philosophy of science literature on simulation; experimental philosophy methodology; recent work on agent-based modeling in philosophy
- Key terms: ["simulation ethics", "computer simulation philosophy", "agent-based modeling normative", "synthetic experiments", "artificial societies", "computational social science philosophy", "digital experiments ethics"]
- Expected papers: 8-12 key papers (smaller, more specialized literature)
- Inclusion criteria: Papers addressing epistemic or ethical status of simulations in normative/philosophical inquiry, not purely technical validation papers

**Relevance to Project**: Methodological foundation. If we're treating Magentic as a social laboratory, we need to understand what makes simulated social experiments legitimate sources of normative insight.

**Search Strategy**:

## Coverage Rationale

These seven domains provide comprehensive coverage across three dimensions:

1. **Theoretical Foundations** (Domains 1, 2): Core normative frameworks (social experimentalism, procedural justice) that ground the project's approach

2. **Application Domain** (Domains 3, 4): Specific literatures on AI ethics and agentic markets that provide context for the empirical investigation

3. **Methodological & Critical** (Domains 5, 6, 7): Methodological justification (simulation ethics, experimental philosophy) and critical tools (moral pathologies) for rigorous analysis

The domains are interdependent: Domain 1 provides the meta-theory; Domain 2 supplies normative criteria; Domains 3-4 describe the application context; Domain 5 justifies the method; Domain 6 provides diagnostic concepts; Domain 7 addresses methodological objections. Together they establish the theoretical foundations, empirical context, methodological legitimacy, and critical tools needed for the proposed research.

## Expected Gaps

Based on this decomposition, we anticipate the following gaps that the research could fill:

1. **Integration gap**: While social experimentalism, AI ethics, and market design exist separately, there is little work applying experimentalist frameworks to AI-mediated markets specifically

2. **Methodological gap**: Limited work on how computer simulations of artificial agents can serve as legitimate social experiments (not just predictive models)

3. **Procedural gap for artificial agents**: Theories of procedural justice largely assume human participants; unclear how they apply when decision-makers are AI proxies

4. **Institutional design gap**: Few concrete proposals for procedural reforms in agentic markets based on normative theory

5. **Hybrid systems gap**: RQ4 points to hybrid human-AI systems, which are under-theorized in both experimentalist and AI ethics literatures
